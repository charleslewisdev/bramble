The Bog Garden

Carnivorous plant display · tiered container concept · cement slab placement

The Concept

A living insect trap, beautiful enough to be a centrepiece

A tiered cedar or weathered concrete tower of sealed bog planters, elevated to counter height to keep it away from dogs and small hands. The Saracenia you already have forms the architectural backbone — add sundews for delicate texture and a cobra lily as a dramatic focal point. Fed by collected rainwater from the gazebo, it becomes genuinely self-sustaining through Portland's wet season and needs only top-up attention in summer.

Placed near the bird pond in a corner of the cement slab, it anchors a micro-habitat cluster — water, bog, insects, birds — that makes the whole patio feel alive.

Layout Options

Three configurations to consider

⭐ Recommended
Three-Tier Tower
Three sealed planters stacked on a cedar stand, ~3ft total height. Water drains slowly between tiers by gravity — bottom stays wettest for sundews, middle for Saracenia, top for cobra lily. Most dramatic and space-efficient.
Wide Tray on Stand
Single large sealed tray (18–24" wide) elevated on a cedar or metal stand to counter height. All species share one bog environment — simpler to build and maintain, slightly less dramatic vertically.
Wall-Mounted Tiers
Three planters bracketed to the house wall in a staggered cascade. Saves floor space entirely, dramatic against the wall. Requires waterproofing the wall attachment points. Best if slab space is premium.

Plant Selection

Four species, one ecosystem

Saracenia — Pitcher Plant
Saracenia purpurea / leucophylla
Your existing plant — the anchor. Upright tubular pitchers trap and digest insects. Hardy to -20°F, fully winter-hardy in Portland. Mid-tier placement. Will multiply slowly into a clump over years.
carnivorous insect trap
Cobra Lily
Darlingtonia californica
Oregon and Northern California native — genuinely prehistoric looking, hooded pitchers with forked "tongue." Dramatic focal point at 18–24". Prefers cool roots — top tier with shade works well. Increasingly available at Portland native nurseries.
carnivorous PNW native focal plant
Sundew
Drosera rotundifolia
Native PNW bog plant, delicate rosettes covered in glistening sticky tentacles that trap small insects. Tiny but mesmerising up close. Perfect bottom-tier filler — loves the wettest conditions. Tiny white flowers in summer.
carnivorous PNW native
Bog Rosemary
Andromeda polifolia
Optional non-carnivorous companion for visual softness — low evergreen shrublet with tiny pink bell flowers in spring, looks beautiful draping over a bog tray edge. Thrives in exactly the same sphagnum/peat conditions as the carnivores.
companion plant

Materials

What you'll need to build the three-tier version

3× Cedar planters (sealed inside) Untreated cedar boxes, 12–16" wide each. Seal interior with pond liner or food-safe waterproof sealant — not wood stain. Drainage hole between tiers only, not to ground.
Sphagnum moss + peat mix 50/50 sphagnum moss and peat. No potting soil, no fertilizer, no amendments. Carnivorous plants need poor, acidic, nutrient-free substrate — standard potting mix will kill them.
Cedar or steel stand Bring bottom tier to ~30" height. Cedar weathers beautifully. Powder-coated steel works well and feels more modern if preferred.
Small overflow tube Between tiers — simple gravity drip connection so water slowly moves from cobra lily tier down to sundew tier, keeping lower tiers wetter automatically.
Rainwater barrel (1 needed) See rain collection note below. 50–100 gallon barrel is plenty for summer top-ups of a small bog garden. Never use tap water.
Decorative top dressing Living sphagnum moss as a top layer looks beautiful and keeps moisture consistent. Add a few smooth river rocks from your existing stash for a natural finish.

Rain Collection

💧 Guttering the Gazebo

Your gazebo covering almost certainly has runoff pooling somewhere — that's free, perfectly pH-neutral rainwater ideal for carnivorous plants. A simple add-on gutter along the low edge of the gazebo roof, routed via downspout into a 50-gallon rain barrel, would collect enough water in a single Portland rainstorm to supply the bog garden for weeks in summer.

The spots in your yard that collect too much moisture are a related problem — if those areas are near the gazebo or fence line, a French drain or simple gravel trench routed toward a rain barrel or away from the foundation may be worth investigating. Worth noting where exactly those wet spots are next time it rains heavily so we can think through drainage properly.

Key note: Gazebo fabric or polycarbonate roofing is ideal — no tar, no treated wood shingles that could leach chemicals into the collection. If your gazebo top is fabric or plastic, you're good. If it's treated wood shakes, let the first few rains flush through before using it for the bog.

Placement

🪴 Where on the slab

Ideal spot is the corner of the cement slab nearest the bird pond — this clusters your water feature, bog garden, and bird activity into one habitat zone that feels intentional rather than scattered. The elevated tower keeps it visible from the pavilion seating area and out of reach of dogs and the baby.

Avoid the pergola corner — that area gets too much competition for light and visual attention from the clematis. The bog garden deserves its own moment.

Partial sun to bright shade suits all four plants here — your east-facing slab gets good morning light which is plenty. Cobra lily actually prefers some afternoon shade, so this exposure is nearly perfect.
